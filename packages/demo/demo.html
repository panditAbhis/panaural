v1.1.0 FIX

We fixed the frequency resolution — here's what that means

The previous version used a frame size of 1,024 samples. At 8,000 Hz sample rate, that made each frequency "bucket" 7.8 Hz wide — like trying to read a ruler that only has markings every 8 millimetres.

Pump bearing defects create sidebands (extra tones) that sit 1–5 Hz apart from the main tone. At 7.8 Hz resolution, those sidebands merged into one blob. You'd see a healthy pump and a failing pump look identical.

We changed the frame size to 8,000 samples. Now each bucket is 0.98 Hz wide. Sidebands are visible. The spectrogram now behaves like the reference instruments (scipy, MATLAB, SpectraPlus).

Before: frameSize=1024 → 8000/1024 = 7.8 Hz/bin ✗ misses pump sidebands
After: frameSize=8000 → 8000/8000 = 1.0 Hz/bin ✓ resolves sidebands correctly
What am I looking at? (click to expand)
A spectrogram is a map of sound over time.

Left → Right = Time. Each column is one tiny slice of your recording, about 1 second wide.

Bottom → Top = Frequency (pitch). Deep, low rumbles live at the bottom. High-pitched whines live at the top. The middle is where most pump hums sit (50–500 Hz).

Colour = Loudness (in dB). Near-white is loud. Black is silence. The inferno colour scale (black → purple → red → orange → white) is an industry standard used by MATLAB, SpectraPlus, and scientific instruments. It makes quiet detail visible without blowing out bright peaks.

A healthy pump makes straight horizontal lines. The lines stay at the same frequency, same brightness, second after second. When a bearing wears, those lines drift upward or new sidebands appear beside them. That drift is what Panaural is trained to catch.
