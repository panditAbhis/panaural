🔊 1 · What is sound — and how does a computer hear it? Foundation

Sound is a pressure wave. When a pump's impeller spins, it pushes and pulls the air (or water) around it millions of times per second, creating alternating high-pressure and low-pressure zones that travel outward like ripples in a pond.

Analogy — the ripple pond: Drop a stone into still water. Ripples spread outward in rings. The height of each ripple is amplitude (loudness). How many rings pass a fixed point per second is frequency (pitch). Sound works the same way, but in air, in all directions, at 343 m/s.

From air pressure to numbers (how your microphone works)

  1. Microphone diaphragm — a thin membrane that moves in and out as pressure waves hit it. Converts pressure → mechanical movement.
  2. Transducer — converts mechanical movement → electrical voltage. A condenser mic changes capacitance; a dynamic mic uses electromagnetic induction.
  3. ADC (Analogue-to-Digital Converter) — samples that voltage at regular intervals (e.g., 8,000 times per second) and rounds each sample to the nearest integer in a fixed range (e.g., -32,768 to +32,767 for 16-bit). This gives us a list of numbers — the WAV file.

Key parameters of a WAV file

Sample rate (SR): 8,000 Hz = 8,000 samples per second = snapshots of pressure
Bit depth: 16-bit = 2¹⁶ = 65,536 possible pressure levels per sample
Duration: samples ÷ SR = 24,000 ÷ 8,000 = 3.0 seconds
Nyquist limit: SR ÷ 2 = 4,000 Hz = highest frequency we can capture

A healthy pump at 50 Hz supply creates vibration at 50 Hz, 100 Hz, 150 Hz (harmonics), and higher bearing frequencies. All of these get captured as pressure waves, picked up by the mic, and stored as 8,000 numbers per second.

🌈 2 · Fourier's theorem — the idea that changed everything (1807) The Big Idea

In 1807, French mathematician Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier made a claim so bold that the scientific establishment initially rejected it: any periodic signal, no matter how complex, can be expressed as a sum of simple sine and cosine waves at different frequencies and amplitudes.

Analogy — the prism: White light looks like one thing. Pass it through a glass prism and it splits into a rainbow — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet. The prism doesn't change the light; it reveals that white light was always a mixture of those colours. The FFT is a mathematical prism. A complex pump sound looks like one noisy signal. The FFT reveals it's always been a mixture of discrete tones — 50 Hz shaft rotation, 200 Hz blade pass, 640 Hz bearing defect.

Why this matters for machines

Different machine faults create different frequency signatures:

  • Shaft imbalance → strong peak exactly at shaft rotation frequency (e.g., 25 Hz for 1500 RPM)
  • Misalignment → strong peak at 2× shaft frequency (50 Hz)
  • Blade wear → peak at blade-pass frequency (shaft × number of blades)
  • Bearing defect → peaks at BPFI/BPFO/BSF/FTF frequencies (complex formula involving geometry)
  • Cavitation → broadband noise rise above 2 kHz

Fourier's theorem says we can separate all of these from a single recording. That's the entire premise of acoustic condition monitoring.

Analogy — the orchestra: Imagine 50 musicians playing simultaneously. To the human ear it sounds like "music." But a skilled conductor can close their eyes and say "the third violin is slightly flat." Fourier analysis does this mathematically — even when all machine faults play at once, it isolates each frequency contribution.
⚡ 3 · The FFT algorithm — how the math actually works (Cooley-Tukey, 1965) Algorithm

The Fourier Transform turns N time samples into N frequency components. The naive Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) computes every output by summing over every input: that's N² multiplications — for 8,000 samples that's 64 million operations per frame.

The Cooley-Tukey insight (divide and conquer)

In 1965, James Cooley and John Tukey (independently rediscovering Gauss's 1805 work) noticed: you can split an N-point DFT into two N/2-point DFTs of the even and odd samples, then combine with a "twiddle factor." Recurse until you have N 1-point DFTs (trivial). Result: O(N log N) instead of O(N²).

N = 8192 samples (our fftSize) Naive DFT: 8192² = 67,108,864 multiplications per frame Cooley-Tukey: 8192 × log₂(8192) = 8192 × 13 = 106,496 multiplications Speedup: 630× faster — the difference between 10 seconds and 16ms per frame

The "butterfly" operation

At each recursion level, Cooley-Tukey combines pairs of values with a complex exponential (the twiddle factor). Each pair looks like a butterfly diagram — two inputs, two outputs — hence the name. This code's fftInPlace(re, im) function implements this exactly:

Step 1: Bit-reversal permutation (reorder inputs so recursion is in-place)
Step 2: For each stage (len=2,4,8,...,N):
For each butterfly: compute twiddle factor, update pair of complex numbers
Result: Frequency domain — re[k] + i·im[k] is the complex amplitude at frequency k·SR/N

This is identical to what MATLAB's fft(), scipy's scipy.fft.fft(), and every other major tool uses. No divergence in the math.

From FFT output to dB magnitude

magnitude[k] = √(re[k]² + im[k]²) / N (normalise by frame size)
dB[k] = 20 × log₁₀(magnitude[k]) (convert to decibels)

We discard the second half (bins N/2+1 to N-1) — they're mirror images of bins 0 to N/2
(the spectrum is symmetric for real-valued signals).
Result: numBins = N/2 + 1 = 4097 bins, each representing SR/N = 0.977 Hz of bandwidth
🪟 4 · Window functions — why we taper the signal before every FFT Signal Processing

The FFT assumes your signal is periodic — that it repeats forever. But your 8,000-sample frame is a finite chunk cut from a continuous signal. At the edges of that chunk, the signal almost certainly doesn't start and end at exactly the same value.

Analogy — the photo edge: Imagine filming a spinning fan. If you start recording mid-rotation and stop mid-rotation, the jump between the last frame and the implied "first frame of the next loop" creates a discontinuity. To the FFT, that discontinuity looks like broadband noise — it smears energy from the fan's rotation frequency into every other frequency bin. This is spectral leakage. A window function is like fading the film in and out smoothly — no hard edge, no artificial jump.

The four window types in Panaural

Hann

0.5·(1 − cos(2πn/N))

Smooth cosine taper to zero. Best all-round choice. Sidelobe suppression ~31.5 dB. Use this for pump monitoring.

Hamming

0.54 − 0.46·cos(2πn/N)

Doesn't taper fully to zero. Better main-lobe narrowness. Good for speech, slightly worse for tonal signals.

Blackman

0.42 − 0.5·cos + 0.08·cos(2θ)

Aggressive taper. Sidelobe ~58 dB. Best when a faint tone sits next to a much louder one (>40 dB dynamic difference). Wider main lobe = slightly coarser resolution.

Rectangular

w[n] = 1.0

No taper. Best time resolution, worst leakage. Correct choice for transient events (clicks, impacts). Do not use for continuous pump tones.

Why Hann is the default

For pump monitoring, the signal is tonal — discrete, persistent frequencies. The Hann window keeps those tones sharp (narrow main lobe) while suppressing leakage enough (31.5 dB) that a blade-pass tone won't mask a bearing sideband 3 Hz away. It's the default in MATLAB's spectrogram() and scipy's stft().

📊 5 · STFT — turning a 1D sound into a 2D map of time and frequency Core Method

The FFT tells you what frequencies exist across the entire recording. But if a fault appears at t=45s and you're analysing 60s of audio, the FFT dilutes that fault across the whole signal. You need to see when frequencies change, not just if.

The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) solves this by sliding a window through time, computing an FFT at each position, and stacking the results into a 2D grid:

For each time position t (step = hopSize samples apart): 1. Extract frameSize samples centred at t 2. Apply window function 3. Compute FFT → frequency spectrum for this moment 4. Stack it as a column in the 2D spectrogram Result: rows = frequency bins (0 → Nyquist) columns = time frames colour = magnitude at that frequency at that time

The fundamental trade-off (Heisenberg uncertainty in signal processing)

You cannot have perfect time resolution AND perfect frequency resolution simultaneously. This is not a software limitation — it's physics (the time-frequency uncertainty principle).

Frequency resolution = SR / frameSize = 8000 / 8000 = 1.0 Hz/bin Time resolution = frameSize / SR = 8000 / 8000 = 1.0 s/frame With 75% overlap (hopSize = 2000): New frame every 2000/8000 = 0.25 s → smoother spectrogram, same frequency resolution (Overlap adds smoothness but doesn't improve the fundamental trade-off)
Analogy — the camera exposure: Long exposure = motion blur (poor time resolution) but captures faint stars (good sensitivity/frequency resolution). Short exposure = freezes motion (good time resolution) but misses dim stars (poor frequency resolution). Our 1-second frame is a "long exposure" — excellent for seeing faint pump tones, but we won't know if a fault appeared at exactly 14:22:31 vs 14:22:30.

What you see in the spectrogram

  • Horizontal bright line, constant position: Steady tone. Healthy pump producing this frequency consistently.
  • Horizontal line that drifts upward over weeks: Blade-pass rising — impeller wear.
  • New sidebands appearing beside a main tone: Bearing fault developing. The sideband spacing equals the bearing defect frequency.
  • Broadband brightness rise above 1 kHz: Cavitation — bubbles collapsing create wideband noise.
  • Diagonal stripe: A transient event — someone slammed a door, a pressure surge.
📏 6 · Decibels (dB) — why sound is measured on a logarithmic scale Units

Human hearing spans a pressure range of roughly 20 μPa (faintest audible sound) to 20 Pa (pain threshold) — a factor of one million. If we used a linear scale, the faintest pump bearing sideband (at, say, 0.001 Pa) would be invisible next to the main 50 Hz shaft tone (at 0.1 Pa). The logarithmic decibel scale compresses this range.

dB = 20 × log₁₀(amplitude / reference) In Panaural: reference = full-scale digital (the maximum value the ADC can produce) So: -20 dB = amplitude is 1/10th of full scale -40 dB = amplitude is 1/100th of full scale -60 dB = amplitude is 1/1000th of full scale -80 dB = amplitude is 1/10,000th of full scale (typical noise floor)

Key dB facts for pump monitoring

  • +6 dB ≈ signal doubled in amplitude. ISO 20816-3 flags Class C→D transition at +6 dB above baseline → this is Panaural's default alert threshold.
  • −3 dB = amplitude at 70.7% of reference (half power). "Half power point" in filter design.
  • The colour scale in this spectrogram maps dB floor (default −80) → dB ceiling (default −10) to black → white. Adjust the ceiling down until bright colours appear on your signal.
  • Our dB values are dBFS (Full Scale), not dB SPL (Sound Pressure Level). They're relative to the ADC maximum, not to the physical 20 μPa reference. You cannot compare our readings to a calibrated sound level meter without calibration.
Analogy — the earthquake Richter scale: A magnitude 7 earthquake is not 2× stronger than magnitude 6 — it's 10× stronger (log₁₀ scale). Similarly, a bearing sideband at −55 dB is not slightly quieter than the −25 dB main tone — it's 1,000× quieter in amplitude (30 dB difference, 10^(30/20) ≈ 32×). Without the log scale you would never see the sideband.
👻 7 · Nyquist theorem and aliasing — why ghost frequencies appear (and how to prevent them) Sampling Theory

The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem (1949) states: to accurately represent a signal containing frequencies up to F Hz, you must sample at a rate of at least 2F samples per second.

Analogy — the wagon wheel effect in old films: A wagon wheel spinning at 12 revolutions per second, filmed at 24 frames per second (2× the rotation speed — exactly at Nyquist) looks like it's standing still. Filmed at 20 fps (below Nyquist), the wheel appears to spin backwards. That backwards rotation is an alias — a ghost frequency that doesn't exist in reality, created by sampling too slowly. The same happens in audio: a 5,000 Hz tone recorded at 8,000 Hz SR appears as a 3,000 Hz ghost (8000 − 5000 = 3000 Hz). You'd think a bearing fault is at 3 kHz when it's actually at 5 kHz.

In Panaural

Sample rate: 8,000 Hz Nyquist: 4,000 Hz ← highest frequency we can represent without aliasing Coverage: Most pump fundamentals (10–500 Hz) → captured correctly Bearing defects can reach 1,000–2,000 Hz → still captured correctly Cavitation (above 2,000 Hz) → partially captured, some aliased The validate.ts module refuses to start if: sampleRate < 2 × max frequency of interest

Anti-aliasing filters

Professional instruments include a hardware low-pass filter that removes all frequencies above Nyquist before the ADC samples. Without this, any high-frequency energy (fan harmonics, electrical noise, ultrasound) aliases into the measurement band. Panaural currently has no anti-aliasing filter — it relies on the source having minimal energy above 4 kHz, which is usually true for pipe-mounted acoustic measurements but is not guaranteed.

⚙️ 8 · Sidebands and bearing fault frequencies — what Panaural is actually looking for Application

This is the "so what?" that connects all the signal processing to a real machine fault.

How a healthy bearing sounds

A rolling element bearing produces a pure tone at the shaft rotation frequency and its harmonics. If a pump has 4 impeller blades spinning at 25 Hz (1500 RPM), the blade-pass frequency is 25 × 4 = 100 Hz. In the spectrogram: one bright line at 100 Hz, steady, constant brightness.

What happens when a bearing starts to fail

A small defect (spall, pit, crack) on the inner or outer race creates a tiny impact each time a rolling element passes over it. That impact repeats at a specific rate — the bearing defect frequency — which depends on bearing geometry. This periodic impact amplitude-modulates the main tone.

Analogy — the drummer with a limp: A healthy drummer hits every beat exactly. A drummer with a sore knee has a slight hesitation every few beats — the rhythm is no longer perfectly steady. In frequency terms, that irregularity creates sidebands (extra frequencies) above and below the main beat frequency. A bearing defect does the same to the shaft tone: the main tone stays, but sidebands appear on both sides at the defect frequency distance.

Bearing fault frequencies (BPFI/BPFO)

Given: shaft speed N Hz, n rolling elements, contact angle α, pitch diameter D, ball diameter d BPFI (inner race) = (n/2) × N × (1 + d/D × cos α) ← most common early fault BPFO (outer race) = (n/2) × N × (1 − d/D × cos α) BSF (ball spin) = (D/2d) × N × (1 − (d/D × cos α)²) FTF (cage) = (1/2) × N × (1 − d/D × cos α) Example — typical HVAC pump (4 balls, shaft 25 Hz, D/d ≈ 3, α ≈ 15°): BPFO ≈ 25 × 4/2 × (1 − 0.32) ≈ 34 Hz Sidebands appear at: 100 ± 34, 100 ± 68, 100 ± 102 Hz...

Why 0.98 Hz/bin resolution is critical

BPFO sideband at 34 Hz sits 34 Hz from the blade-pass at 100 Hz. At 7.8 Hz/bin (old default), the sideband is only 4 bins away from the main tone — visible but merges with leakage. If the defect frequency is 3 Hz (slow shaft, many balls), it's completely invisible. At 0.98 Hz/bin, even 1 Hz sidebands are distinct bins. This is why the resolution fix in v1.1.0 was not cosmetic — it was fundamental.

🧪 How are the self-tests designed? (click to see the synthetic signal methodology)

Test 1 — Pure sine detection

Generate a mathematically perfect 50 Hz sine wave at 8,000 Hz sample rate for 1 second. Run STFT with frameSize=8000 (fftSize=8192). Check that the peak bin is within ±3 bins of the expected bin (50 × 8192 / 8000 = bin 51). This confirms the FFT algorithm produces the correct frequency bins for a known input.

Test 2 — Noise robustness (SNR ≈ 10 dB)

Mix the 50 Hz sine with Gaussian noise (Box-Muller transform, std=0.224, SNR≈10 dB). The sine still has 10 dB more power than the noise floor — a realistic SNR for a microphone pressed against a pipe with ambient background noise. If the peak bin still detects at 50 Hz, the algorithm is robust to typical recording conditions.

Test 3 — Silence (no false alarms)

Feed all-zero samples. No bin should exceed −80 dB. If it does, the FFT has numerical noise (floating point accumulation errors). −240 dB (observed) means the implementation is numerically clean.

Test 4 — Clipping detection

Generate a sine at 2× amplitude, then hard-clip at ±0.9. Over 71% of samples will be at the clipping boundary. The test checks this is detected and warned. Clipping (ADC saturation) distorts the entire spectrum — harmonics appear at every frequency, masking real faults. The system must warn, not silently compute wrong results.
