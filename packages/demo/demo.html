Understanding the technology
1 — What is sound and how does a computer hear it? Foundation

Sound is a pressure wave. When a pump impeller spins, it pushes and pulls surrounding air millions of times per second, creating alternating high-pressure and low-pressure zones that travel outward like ripples on water.

The ripple analogy: Drop a stone into still water. Ripples spread outward. The height of each ripple is amplitude (loudness). How many ripple peaks pass a fixed point per second is frequency (pitch). Sound works the same way, but in air, at 343 m/s.

From air pressure to numbers

  1. Microphone diaphragm — a thin membrane that moves in/out as pressure waves hit it. Converts pressure to mechanical motion.
  2. Transducer — converts motion to electrical voltage. Condenser mics change capacitance; dynamic mics use electromagnetic induction.
  3. ADC (Analogue-to-Digital Converter) — samples the voltage at fixed intervals and rounds each sample to the nearest integer. 8,000 times per second at 16-bit depth gives a list of numbers — that is the WAV file.

Key parameters of the WAV file in this demo

Sample rate: 8,000 Hz = 8,000 snapshots of pressure per second Bit depth: 16-bit = 2^16 = 65,536 possible pressure levels per sample Duration: samples / SR = 24,000 / 8,000 = 3.0 seconds Nyquist: SR / 2 = 4,000 Hz = highest frequency capturable without aliasing

A pump at 50 Hz supply creates vibration at 50 Hz, 100 Hz, 150 Hz (harmonics), and higher bearing frequencies. All are captured as pressure waves, converted to numbers, stored in the WAV.

2 — Fourier's theorem — the idea that changed everything (1807) The Big Idea

In 1807, Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier proved: any periodic signal, no matter how complex, can be expressed as a sum of simple sine and cosine waves at different frequencies and amplitudes. The establishment initially rejected the claim. It was correct.

The prism analogy: White light looks like one thing. A glass prism splits it into a rainbow — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet. The prism does not change the light; it reveals that white light was always a mixture of those colours. The FFT is a mathematical prism. A complex pump sound looks like one noisy signal. The FFT reveals it was always a mixture of discrete tones — 50 Hz shaft rotation, 200 Hz blade-pass, 640 Hz bearing defect.

Why this matters for machines

Different faults produce different frequency signatures:

  • Shaft imbalance — strong peak at shaft rotation frequency (e.g., 25 Hz for 1500 RPM)
  • Misalignment — strong peak at 2× shaft frequency
  • Blade wear — peak at blade-pass frequency (shaft Hz × number of blades)
  • Bearing defect — peaks at BPFI/BPFO/BSF/FTF (geometry-dependent formula)
  • Cavitation — broadband noise rise above 1–2 kHz

Fourier's theorem says all of these can be separated from a single recording. That is the entire premise of acoustic condition monitoring.

The orchestra analogy: Fifty musicians playing simultaneously sounds like "music." A skilled conductor closes their eyes and says "the third violin is flat." Fourier analysis does this mathematically — even when all machine faults play at once, it isolates each contribution.
3 — The FFT algorithm — Cooley-Tukey, 1965 Algorithm

The Fourier Transform turns N time samples into N frequency components. The naive Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) computes every output by summing over every input: N² multiplications. For 8,000 samples that is 64 million operations per frame.

The Cooley-Tukey insight

In 1965, James Cooley and John Tukey (independently rediscovering Gauss's 1805 work) noticed: split an N-point DFT into two N/2-point DFTs of the even and odd samples, then combine with a "twiddle factor." Recurse until you have N trivial 1-point DFTs. Result: O(N log N) instead of O(N²).

N = 8192 samples (our fftSize, next power of 2 above 8000) Naive DFT: 8192² = 67,108,864 multiplications Cooley-Tukey: 8192 × log&sub2;(8192) = 106,496 multiplications Speedup: 630× faster — difference between 10 seconds and 16 ms per frame

The butterfly operation

At each recursion level, pairs of values are combined with a complex exponential (twiddle factor). Each pair looks like a butterfly diagram — two inputs, two outputs. The fftInPlace(re, im) function in this page implements this exactly: bit-reversal permutation, then staged butterfly passes. This is identical to what MATLAB's fft() and scipy's fft.fft() use.

From FFT output to dB magnitude

magnitude[k] = sqrt(re[k]² + im[k]²) / N ← normalise by frame size dB[k] = 20 × log&sub10;(magnitude[k]) ← convert to decibels Discard bins N/2+1 to N-1: mirror images for real-valued signals Result: numBins = N/2 + 1 = 4097 bins, each spanning SR/N = 0.977 Hz
4 — Window functions — why we taper the signal before every FFT Signal Processing

The FFT assumes the signal is periodic — that it repeats forever. Your 8,000-sample frame is a finite chunk cut from a continuous signal. At the frame edges the signal almost certainly does not start and end at the same value. This jump looks like broadband noise to the FFT and smears energy from one frequency into all others. This is spectral leakage.

The camera analogy: Filming a spinning fan: if recording starts and stops mid-rotation, the jump between last and first frame creates an artificial discontinuity. To the FFT, that discontinuity appears as broadband noise. A window function is like fading the film in and out smoothly at both ends — no hard edge, no artificial jump.

The four window types in Panaural

Hann (default)

0.5 × (1 − cos(2πn/N))

Smooth cosine taper to zero. Sidelobe suppression ~31.5 dB. Best all-round for tonal signals. Use for pump monitoring.

Hamming

0.54 − 0.46cos(2πn/N)

Does not taper to zero. Slightly narrower main lobe than Hann. Better for speech; slightly worse for isolated tones.

Blackman

0.42 − 0.5cos + 0.08cos(2θ)

Aggressive taper. Sidelobe ~58 dB. Best when a faint tone sits >40 dB below a loud one. Wider main lobe = slightly coarser resolution.

Rectangular

w[n] = 1.0

No taper. Best time resolution, worst leakage. Correct for transient events (impacts). Do not use for continuous pump tones.

5 — STFT — turning a 1D signal into a 2D time-frequency map Core Method

A plain FFT tells you what frequencies exist across the whole recording but not when they appear. The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) solves this by sliding a window through time, computing an FFT at each position, and stacking the results into a grid.

For each time position t (advancing by hopSize samples): 1. Extract frameSize samples starting at t 2. Apply window function (Hann) 3. Compute FFT → frequency spectrum for this moment in time 4. Stack it as one column in the 2D grid Result: rows = frequency bins (0 Hz to Nyquist) columns = time frames colour = magnitude in dB at that frequency at that time

The fundamental trade-off

You cannot have perfect time resolution and perfect frequency resolution simultaneously. This is not a software limitation — it is the time-frequency uncertainty principle.

Frequency resolution = SR / frameSize = 8000 / 8000 = 1.0 Hz/bin Time resolution = frameSize / SR = 8000 / 8000 = 1.0 s/frame With 75% overlap (hopSize = 2000): New frame every 2000/8000 = 0.25 s → smoother display (overlap improves visual smoothness but does not change the fundamental resolution)
The camera exposure analogy: Long exposure captures faint stars (high sensitivity) but blurs moving objects (poor time resolution). Short exposure freezes motion but misses dim stars. Our 1-second frame is a long exposure — excellent for faint pump tones, but we will not pinpoint a fault to the exact second it appeared.

What to look for in the spectrogram

  • Horizontal bright line, constant: Steady tone. Healthy machine component.
  • Line that slowly drifts upward over days/weeks: Frequency rising — e.g., blade-pass rising as impeller wears.
  • New faint lines appearing beside a main tone: Sidebands. Bearing fault developing.
  • Broadband brightness above 1 kHz: Cavitation — bubble collapse creates wideband noise.
  • Diagonal stripe: Transient — pressure surge, door slam, external impact.
6 — Decibels — why sound uses a logarithmic scale Units

Human hearing spans pressure from 20 µPa (faintest audible) to 20 Pa (pain threshold) — a ratio of one million. A linear scale would make faint bearing sidebands invisible beside the dominant shaft tone. The decibel compresses this range logarithmically.

dB = 20 × log&sub10;(amplitude / reference) In Panaural: reference = ADC full scale (maximum recordable value) 0 dB = full scale (maximum) -20 dB = 1/10 of full scale -40 dB = 1/100 of full scale -60 dB = 1/1,000 of full scale -80 dB = 1/10,000 of full scale (typical noise floor for 16-bit audio)

Key facts for pump monitoring

  • +6 dB = amplitude doubled. ISO 20816-3 flags Class C→D transition at +6 dB above baseline. This is Panaural's default alert threshold.
  • Our values are dBFS (Full Scale), not dB SPL (Sound Pressure Level). Cannot compare to a calibrated sound meter without a calibration step.
  • The colour scale maps dB floor (default −80) to dB ceiling (default −10). If the spectrogram looks dark, lower the ceiling control.
The earthquake analogy: Richter magnitude 7 is not twice as strong as magnitude 6 — it is 10× stronger (log scale). A bearing sideband at −55 dB is not slightly quieter than a −25 dB main tone — it is 1,000× quieter in amplitude. Without the log scale the sideband would be invisible.
7 — Nyquist theorem and aliasing — why ghost frequencies appear Sampling Theory

The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem states: to represent a signal containing frequencies up to F Hz, you must sample at least 2F times per second. Violate this and you get aliasing — frequencies that appear to be at the wrong location in the spectrum.

The wagon wheel effect: A wagon wheel spinning at 12 revolutions per second, filmed at 20 frames per second (below Nyquist for the rotation), appears to spin backwards. That backwards rotation is an alias — a ghost frequency that does not exist in reality. In audio: a 5,000 Hz tone recorded at 8,000 Hz sample rate appears as a ghost at 3,000 Hz (8000 − 5000). You would think a bearing fault is at 3 kHz when it is actually at 5 kHz.
Sample rate: 8,000 Hz Nyquist: 4,000 Hz ← highest frequency representable without aliasing Pump fundamentals (10–500 Hz): captured correctly Bearing defects (up to ~2,000 Hz): captured correctly Cavitation (above 2,000 Hz): partially captured (some aliasing possible near Nyquist) validate.ts refuses to start if: sampleRate < 2 × max frequency of interest

Anti-aliasing filters

Professional instruments include a hardware low-pass filter that removes all frequencies above Nyquist before the ADC samples. Without this, any high-frequency energy (fan harmonics, electrical noise, ultrasound) aliases into the measurement band. Panaural currently has no hardware anti-aliasing filter — this is an open gap documented in the audit above.

8 — Sidebands and bearing fault frequencies — what Panaural looks for Application

A healthy bearing

A rolling-element bearing produces tones at the shaft rotation frequency and its harmonics. A pump with 4 impeller blades at 25 Hz shaft speed produces blade-pass at 25 × 4 = 100 Hz. In the spectrogram: one steady bright line at 100 Hz, constant brightness over time.

What happens when a bearing fails

A defect (spall, pit, crack) on a bearing race creates a small impact each time a rolling element passes over it. That impact repeats at the bearing defect frequency, which amplitude-modulates the main tone. Modulation creates sidebands — extra frequency components above and below the main tone, spaced by the defect frequency.

The drummer analogy: A healthy drummer hits every beat exactly. A drummer with a sore knee has a slight hesitation every few beats — the rhythm is no longer perfectly steady. In frequency terms, that irregularity creates sidebands above and below the beat frequency. A bearing defect does the same to the main shaft tone.

Bearing fault frequency formulas

Given: shaft speed N Hz, n rolling elements, contact angle α, pitch diameter D, ball diameter d BPFI (inner race) = (n/2) × N × (1 + d/D × cosα) ← most common early fault BPFO (outer race) = (n/2) × N × (1 − d/D × cosα) BSF (ball spin) = (D/2d) × N × (1 − (d/D × cosα)²) FTF (cage) = (1/2) × N × (1 − d/D × cosα) Example — HVAC pump (4 balls, 25 Hz shaft, D/d ≈ 3, α ≈ 15°): BPFO ≈ 34 Hz Sidebands appear at: 100 ± 34, 100 ± 68, 100 ± 102 Hz...

Why 0.98 Hz/bin resolution is critical

If the defect frequency is 3 Hz (slow shaft, many balls), the sidebands are 3 Hz from the main tone. At 7.8 Hz/bin (the old default), the sideband falls inside the same bin as the main tone — invisible. At 0.98 Hz/bin, it is three bins away — clearly distinct. The resolution fix in v1.1.0 was not cosmetic. It was the difference between detecting a developing fault and missing it entirely.

G1 — Physics of sound — pressure, frequency, amplitude and the human ear Physics

What a sound wave actually is

Air at rest has a uniform pressure of roughly 101,325 Pa (one atmosphere). Sound is a longitudinal pressure disturbance — alternating regions of compression (higher pressure) and rarefaction (lower pressure) that propagate outward from the source at 343 m/s at 20 °C.

No air molecule travels with the wave. Each molecule oscillates back and forth around its rest position by a tiny amount, pushing its neighbour, which pushes its neighbour. The energy travels; the matter stays local. Exactly like a crowd wave in a stadium.

The three properties of a pure tone

Frequency f [Hz] — how many pressure cycles per second A pump at 1500 RPM rotates 25 times/second: f = 25 Hz Amplitude A [Pa] — peak pressure deviation from atmospheric Faint bearing sideband: ~0.001 Pa above atmospheric Phase phi [rad] — where in the cycle the tone starts at t=0 Two tones at same f but different phi partially cancel

Human hearing vs machine monitoring

Human ear: 20 Hz – 20 kHz. Most sensitive around 1–4 kHz (speech band). Below 200 Hz we perceive as "rumble." Pump monitoring lives mostly in 10–2,000 Hz — well within hearing, but human ears cannot reliably detect a 0.5 dB amplitude change or a 2 Hz frequency drift. That is why we need the spectrogram.

Why pump faults produce specific frequencies

Any rotating part that contacts another surface at a fixed rate creates a periodic impact. Periodic impacts are tonal. The frequency equals the contact rate. This is why:

  • An imbalanced shaft at 25 Hz produces a strong 25 Hz tone (one heavy side hits air resistance once per revolution)
  • A 4-blade impeller at 25 Hz produces 100 Hz blade-pass (four blades pass the volute tongue per revolution)
  • A bearing with inner race defect produces a tone at BPFI (the defect contacts rolling elements at that rate)

Every machine fault has a kinematic frequency derived from geometry and rotation speed. Acoustic condition monitoring is pattern-matching measured frequencies to kinematic predictions.

Key insight: A healthy machine produces a stable set of tones at predictable frequencies. A developing fault adds new tones or shifts existing ones. The spectrogram's job is to make those changes visible before they become catastrophic.
G2 — Fourier analysis — from the theorem to the transform, step by step Mathematics

The theorem, stated precisely

Any square-integrable function f(t) defined on a finite interval [0, T] can be expressed as an infinite sum of sinusoids whose frequencies are integer multiples of 1/T:

f(t) = a0/2 + sum_n [ a_n * cos(2*pi*n*t/T) + b_n * sin(2*pi*n*t/T) ] Where: a_n = (2/T) * integral_0^T f(t) * cos(2*pi*n*t/T) dt b_n = (2/T) * integral_0^T f(t) * sin(2*pi*n*t/T) dt

The integrals measure how much of each sinusoid is present in f(t). This is the key intuition: the Fourier transform does not add sinusoids to the signal — it discovers the sinusoids that were already there.

The complex exponential form (what computers compute)

Using Euler's formula (e^(i*theta) = cos(theta) + i*sin(theta)), the transform collapses to a single integral over complex exponentials. The DFT is the discrete version of this:

X[k] = sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] * e^(-i * 2*pi * k * n / N) k = frequency bin index (0 to N-1) n = time sample index X[k] = complex number: magnitude = amplitude, angle = phase

Why we discard the second half of the output

For a real-valued signal x[n] (all time-domain samples are real numbers, as they are from any microphone), the DFT output is Hermitian symmetric: X[N-k] = conjugate(X[k]). Bins N/2+1 through N-1 contain no new information. We keep only bins 0 through N/2, giving N/2 + 1 unique frequency components.

Worked example with our test file

Signal: 50 Hz + 150 Hz, sample rate 8000 Hz, N=8000 samples, fftSize=8192 Bin for 50 Hz: round(50 * 8192 / 8000) = round(51.20) = 51 Bin for 150 Hz: round(150 * 8192 / 8000) = round(153.60) = 154 Actual frequency of bin 51: 51 * 8000 / 8192 = 49.805 Hz (error: 0.195 Hz) Actual frequency of bin 154: 154 * 8000 / 8192 = 150.390 Hz (error: 0.390 Hz) scipy fft verification: 50.000 Hz (0.000 error), 150.000 Hz (0.000 error) Note: scipy reports 0.000 error because it interpolates to sub-bin resolution. The bin itself is at 49.8 Hz. The error is 0.2 Hz, within 1 Hz ISO tolerance.
Analogy — the radio tuner: An FM radio tuner sweeps through frequencies looking for energy at each frequency. At 98.5 MHz it finds your favourite station. The DFT does the same thing digitally: it tests every integer-multiple-of-(SR/N) frequency and measures how much energy is there. The difference is that DFT tests all frequencies simultaneously in O(N log N) operations.
G3 — FFT implementation — reading the actual code in this page Implementation

Step 1 — Bit-reversal permutation

Cooley-Tukey recursively splits the input into even-indexed and odd-indexed halves. After log2(N) splits the elements end up in "bit-reversed" order. Rather than actually recursing, we pre-shuffle the array into that order so the algorithm runs in-place (no extra memory).

Example for N=8, indices 0..7: Decimal: 0 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 Binary: 000 001 010 011 100 101 110 111 Reversed:000 100 010 110 001 101 011 111 Decimal: 0 4 2 6 1 5 3 7 ← new order after bit-reversal

Step 2 — Butterfly stages

After bit-reversal, the algorithm does log2(N) stages. At each stage, length-2 sub-DFTs are combined into length-4 sub-DFTs, then length-8, and so on until the full DFT is assembled. Each "butterfly" operation updates two complex numbers:

twiddle W = e^(-2*pi*i*k/len) = cos(-2*pi*k/len) + i*sin(-2*pi*k/len) u = re[i+k] + i*im[i+k] (top input) v = re[i+k+half]*W.re - im[i+k+half]*W.im (bottom input * twiddle) + i*(re[i+k+half]*W.im + im[i+k+half]*W.re) new top = u + v new bottom = u - v

Why fftSize = nextPow2(frameSize)

Cooley-Tukey requires N to be a power of 2. Our frameSize is 8000 (not a power of 2). We zero-pad to 8192 = 2^13. Zero-padding does NOT improve resolution — it just interpolates between the bins you already have. Resolution is fixed by frameSize, not fftSize.

frameSize = 8000 samples → fftSize = nextPow2(8000) = 8192 Resolution = SR / frameSize = 8000 / 8000 = 1.0 Hz/bin (set by frameSize) Bin spacing = SR / fftSize = 8000 / 8192 = 0.977 Hz/bin (slightly different due to zero-pad) ↑ This is why bin 51 corresponds to 49.8 Hz, not exactly 50 Hz

Normalisation

Raw FFT output magnitude depends on N. We divide by N so that a sine wave of amplitude 1.0 produces magnitude 0.5 in the output (energy splits equally between positive and negative frequency bins). We then multiply by 2 for bins 1 through N/2-1 to recover the single-sided amplitude. The code uses mag / fN which is equivalent and matches scipy's default.

G4 — Window functions — the mathematics of spectral leakage and its cure Signal Processing

Where leakage comes from — the convolution theorem

Multiplying a signal by a rectangular window in the time domain is equivalent to convolving its DFT with the DFT of the rectangle (a sinc function) in the frequency domain. The sinc has significant sidelobes — energy at the sidebands gets "smeared" across adjacent bins. A well-designed window has smaller sidelobes at the cost of a wider main lobe.

Quantified trade-offs

Window Main lobe width Peak sidelobe Coherent gain Rectangular 1 bin -13 dB 1.000 Hann 2 bins -31.5 dB 0.500 Hamming 2 bins -42.7 dB 0.540 Blackman 3 bins -58.1 dB 0.420 Flat-top 5 bins -93.0 dB 0.215 (best amplitude accuracy)

Main lobe width: wider means two close-together tones cannot be resolved. Peak sidelobe: if a strong tone at 100 Hz has sidelobes at -31.5 dB, any tone within those bins at less than 31.5 dB relative to the main tone will be masked. Coherent gain: amplitude scale factor — must be compensated when measuring absolute amplitudes (divide measured magnitude by this value).

Which window for pump monitoring and why

Pump signals are tonal (strong, steady, narrow peaks). Main concern: nearby sideband tones being masked by the dominant shaft/blade-pass tone. Hann gives -31.5 dB sidelobe suppression — meaning a bearing sideband that is 30 dB below the main tone is still visible. Bearing sidebands are often 20–40 dB below the main tone. Hann just passes; Blackman is safer if you expect 40+ dB dynamic range. Default: Hann.

Analogy — the spotlight: Without windowing, the FFT sees your signal through a window that is fully bright in the middle and then switches off abruptly at the edges. That sharp switch-off is an "edge effect" that rings across all frequencies. A Hann window is like a spotlight that fades smoothly to black at the edges — no hard cut, no ringing.
G5 — STFT and the time-frequency uncertainty principle — the fundamental trade-off Theory

The uncertainty principle for signals

In quantum mechanics, Heisenberg's uncertainty principle says position and momentum cannot both be known precisely. There is an exact analogue in signal processing: time localisation and frequency resolution cannot both be made arbitrarily small. Their product is bounded:

Delta_t * Delta_f >= 1 / (4*pi) (Gabor's limit, 1946) This is not a technology limitation. It is a mathematical fact about Fourier analysis. For our default settings: Delta_t = frameSize / SR = 8000 / 8000 = 1.0 second Delta_f = SR / frameSize = 8000 / 8000 = 1.0 Hz Product = 1.0 s * 1.0 Hz = 1.0 (well above Gabor's limit of 0.080)

Choosing the right frame size for your application

Application Need Recommended frameSize ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Pump bearing sideband (1 Hz) High freq resolution 8000 (1.0 Hz/bin) Variable-speed machine High time resolution 512 (15.6 Hz/bin) + order tracking Cavitation detection Broadband, time matters 1024 (7.8 Hz/bin) Slow drift monitoring Freq precision dominates 16000+ (0.5 Hz/bin)

Overlap and its effect

Hop size controls how far the window advances per frame. With 75% overlap (hop = frame/4): each time sample is covered by 4 frames. This makes the spectrogram smoother visually but does NOT improve frequency resolution. It does improve time resolution of detected events (you see a transient event in more frames, so you can localise it better in time).

Common mistake: People add overlap to "improve resolution." It does not. Frequency resolution is fixed by frameSize. Overlap only smooths the display and slightly improves event time-localisation. If you need better frequency resolution, increase frameSize. If you need better time localisation, decrease frameSize (accepting coarser frequency resolution).
G6 — Decibels in depth — dBFS vs dB SPL, calibration, and dynamic range Measurement

Three different "dB" systems in common use

dB SPL: reference = 20 micropascals (threshold of human hearing) Used by acoustic engineers, noise regulations, calibrated meters 0 dB SPL = quietest audible sound; 94 dB SPL = 1 Pa; 140 dB SPL = pain dBFS: reference = full scale of the ADC (maximum recordable value) Used in digital audio, software, this tool 0 dBFS = ADC maximum; typical signal: -18 to -6 dBFS; noise floor: -96 dBFS (16-bit) dBV: reference = 1 Volt RMS Used by electronics engineers measuring mic preamp output A microphone might produce -60 dBV at 94 dB SPL (its sensitivity)

Converting between them requires calibration

dB SPL = dBFS + calibration_offset calibration_offset = SPL_reference - dBFS_at_reference = 94 dBFS - (measured dBFS when signal is exactly 94 dB SPL) Example: microphone at 1 Pa (94 dB SPL) produces -18 dBFS in recording calibration_offset = 94 - (-18) = 112 dB Now: measured_SPL = measured_dBFS + 112 Panaural currently has no calibration_offset. Our numbers are dBFS only. This is why we cannot compare readings across different microphones or sessions with different gain settings.

Dynamic range of 16-bit audio

16-bit integer range: -32768 to +32767 (2^16 = 65536 levels) Theoretical dynamic range: 20 * log10(32767) = 90.3 dB Practical dynamic range: ~85 dB (noise floor of typical ADC is -85 to -96 dBFS) For pump monitoring: Blade-pass tone: typically -18 to -6 dBFS Bearing sidebands: typically -50 to -35 dBFS (30-45 dB below main tone) Noise floor: -80 dBFS (good mic in quiet environment) Margin above noise: 30+ dB for sidebands -> detectable with 16-bit audio
Why -10 dBFS ceiling by default: Signals that clip (hit 0 dBFS) distort badly. A -10 dBFS ceiling means we display the top 70 dB of the dynamic range, stopping 10 dB below clipping. If your signal peaks are too faint to see, lower the ceiling to -30 dBFS. If peaks are clipped (flat tops), raise the ceiling toward 0 dBFS.
G7 — Baseline modelling and drift detection — the statistics of "normal" Weekend 2

The spectrogram shows what is happening now. Drift detection answers: is what is happening now different from what was normal? This requires a statistical model of "normal."

Building a baseline

Record N frames (N ≥ 30, ideally ≥ 100) of the machine in confirmed-healthy state. For each frequency bin k, compute the mean and standard deviation across all frames:

For bin k, across N healthy frames: mu[k] = (1/N) * sum_i frame_i[k] ← mean power in dB sigma[k] = sqrt( (1/N) * sum_i (frame_i[k] - mu[k])^2 ) ← natural variation Baseline = { mu[k], sigma[k] } for k = 0..numBins-1 This is a multivariate Gaussian model. Assumption: at each frequency, power is normally distributed around the mean during healthy operation.

What sigma tells you

sigma[k] captures natural variation: small changes due to load fluctuation, temperature, ambient noise. A large sigma at a bin means that frequency varies a lot even when healthy — so a drift must be larger before we flag it. A small sigma means that frequency is very stable — even a small change is suspicious.

Mahalanobis distance — "how unusual is this frame?"

Simple (per-bin z-score): z[k] = (current[k] - mu[k]) / sigma[k] Mahalanobis distance (accounts for correlations between bins): D^2 = (x - mu)^T * C^-1 * (x - mu) Where C is the covariance matrix (sigma[k] on diagonal if bins assumed independent) For N=4097 bins, full C is 4097x4097 = 67M elements -> use diagonal approximation D^2 = sum_k (current[k] - mu[k])^2 / sigma[k]^2 ← simplified Mahalanobis Under the null hypothesis (machine is healthy), D^2 follows a chi-squared distribution with numBins degrees of freedom. At 99.9% confidence: flag if D^2 > chi2_inv(0.999, numBins).
Analogy — the medical blood test: A blood panel measures 20 biomarkers. Each has a reference range (mean + sigma). Mahalanobis distance is like asking "how far is this patient's entire blood panel from the reference, weighted by how much each biomarker normally varies?" A patient with mildly elevated cholesterol AND mildly low iron is more unusual than one with just elevated cholesterol, even if each individual reading is in range. Mahalanobis captures the combination.

CUSUM — detecting slow monotonic drift

Mahalanobis is good at detecting sudden jumps. CUSUM (Cumulative Sum) is better for slow, consistent drift that never triggers a single-frame threshold.

CUSUM algorithm (for one bin k): S_0 = 0 (start at zero) S_t = max(0, S_{t-1} + (current[k] - mu[k] - delta)) delta = "slack" parameter, half the drift magnitude you want to detect Alert when S_t > h (threshold h, typically 4-5 * sigma[k]) Intuition: If machine is healthy: (current[k] - mu[k]) oscillates around 0. Subtracting delta keeps S_t small. Reset to 0 on negative swing. If machine is drifting up: each frame adds a positive increment. S_t climbs steadily until it crosses h. Alert. A drift of 2*delta (2x the slack) triggers alert in roughly h/delta frames.
Analogy — the penny jar: Every day you put some pennies in a jar. Some days you add a few, some days you take some out (healthy variation). The jar stays roughly level. Now the machine starts deteriorating: every day you add slightly more than you remove. The jar slowly fills. After enough days, it overflows — that is the CUSUM alert. It never triggers on a single bad day; it catches the trend.
G8 — Calibration protocol — making measurements comparable across sessions Weekend 2

Why calibration is necessary

You record a pump today with your laptop mic at 50% gain. Next week you record the same pump at 60% gain. The spectrogram looks different — but the pump did not change, your gain setting did. Without calibration, every session is its own reference frame. You cannot compare absolute dBFS levels across sessions.

The reference tone method

Before each measurement session: play a known-frequency reference tone (e.g., 440 Hz at known amplitude) through a speaker near the microphone. Record it for 2 seconds. Measure the dBFS of the 440 Hz bin. The difference between expected and measured is the session offset. Add this offset to all subsequent measurements in that session.

Session A: reference tone at 440 Hz measures -20.0 dBFS Session B: reference tone at 440 Hz measures -17.5 dBFS (2.5 dB louder, mic moved closer) Session B offset: -20.0 - (-17.5) = -2.5 dB Apply to Session B: all_bins_session_B -= 2.5 dB Now sessions A and B are comparable.

Limitations of acoustic calibration

  • Microphone frequency response: The mic may be 3 dB flatter at 440 Hz than at 100 Hz. Calibrating at 440 Hz corrects the gain but not the frequency-dependent response shape.
  • Position sensitivity: Moving the mic 5 cm changes the SPL by 1–3 dB for near-field sources. Tape a reference mark on the pipe.
  • Background noise: If background noise changes between sessions, it corrupts broadband measurements even after gain calibration. Run self-test for silence before each session.
Practical minimum: Always record a reference tone at the start of each monitoring session. Store the session offset in the baseline file. Without this, your "drift detection" may be detecting your laptop battery dying (lower mic gain at low charge) rather than a pump fault.
G9 — Frame quality scoring — rejecting bad data before it corrupts the baseline Weekend 2

Why not all frames are equal

A 3-second recording contains 9 frames at our default settings. During those 9 frames: someone may walk past the microphone, a door closes, a truck drives by. Those frames contain external noise, not machine noise. If included in the baseline, they raise mu and sigma at all frequencies, making the detector less sensitive. Garbage in, garbage out.

Quality criteria for a frame

1. Clipping check: < 1% of samples at +/- 0.9 (else ADC saturated) 2. Silence check: max bin > noise_floor + 20 dB (else no signal) 3. SNR estimate: (peak bin dB) - (median dB across all bins) > 15 dB (else signal buried in noise) 4. Stationarity: RMS of frame within 6 dB of session median (else transient event, reject) 5. Spectral flatness: if spectrum is flat (SFM near 1.0), it is noise not tonal signal

SNR estimation without a reference

Noise floor estimation method: Sort all bin magnitudes. Bottom 20th percentile is likely noise floor. Mean of top 5% of bins is likely signal. SNR_estimate = mean(top_5_pct_bins) - mean(bottom_20_pct_bins) [in dB] A frame with SNR > 15 dB is usable. Below 10 dB: reject. Between 10-15 dB: flag as low quality, include with reduced weight.

Quality score output

Each frame gets a quality score from 0.0 to 1.0. The baseline is built as a weighted average: high-quality frames contribute more. This makes the baseline robust to the occasional noise intrusion without requiring manual review of every frame.

G10 — Uncertainty quantification — knowing what you do not know Weekend 2

Why uncertainty matters more than the number

An alert that says "bin 51 is 3 dB above baseline" is only meaningful if you know the baseline uncertainty. If sigma[51] = 4 dB (high natural variation), 3 dB above baseline is normal. If sigma[51] = 0.5 dB (very stable tone), 3 dB is an emergency. The alert must be expressed in terms of sigma, not absolute dB.

Confidence bands on the spectrogram

At each frequency bin k, the 99.7% confidence band (3-sigma rule) is: Lower: mu[k] - 3 * sigma[k] Upper: mu[k] + 3 * sigma[k] If current[k] > mu[k] + 3*sigma[k]: significant elevation (0.15% false alarm rate) If current[k] > mu[k] + 2*sigma[k]: moderate elevation (2.3% false alarm rate) If current[k] < mu[k] - 3*sigma[k]: significant attenuation (possible sensor fault)

Minimum baseline length for reliable sigma estimates

Standard error of sigma = sigma / sqrt(2*(N-1)) For N=10 frames: SE = sigma / 4.24 (24% error in sigma) For N=30 frames: SE = sigma / 7.68 (13% error in sigma) For N=100 frames: SE = sigma / 14.1 (7% error in sigma) For N=300 frames: SE = sigma / 24.5 (4% error in sigma) Recommendation: minimum 30 frames for operational use. 100+ frames for production baseline. Collect over multiple operating days to capture load variation.
Analogy — the weather forecast: "30% chance of rain" is not a guess. It is a calibrated probability derived from thousands of historical cases with similar atmospheric patterns. Your sigma estimate is similarly calibrated — but only if you have enough historical frames. With 5 frames, your sigma is a rough guess. With 300 frames, it is a reliable characterisation of normal variation.
G11 — Bearing fault frequencies — worked calculation for a real bearing Application

SKF 6205-2RS (common HVAC pump bearing) — full calculation

Bearing data (SKF 6205-2RS): n_balls = 9 (number of rolling elements) d_ball = 7.938 mm (ball diameter) D_pitch = 38.5 mm (pitch circle diameter) alpha = 0 degrees (contact angle, deep groove = 0) cos(alpha) = cos(0) = 1.0 Shaft speed: 1450 RPM = 24.17 Hz (common HVAC pump speed at 50 Hz supply with slip) Fundamental frequencies: BPFI = (n/2) * f_s * (1 + d/D * cos(alpha)) = (9/2) * 24.17 * (1 + 7.938/38.5 * 1.0) = 4.5 * 24.17 * 1.2062 = 131.2 Hz BPFO = (n/2) * f_s * (1 - d/D * cos(alpha)) = 4.5 * 24.17 * (1 - 0.2062) = 4.5 * 24.17 * 0.7938 = 86.4 Hz FTF = (f_s/2) * (1 - d/D * cos(alpha)) = 12.08 * 0.7938 = 9.6 Hz (cage rotation frequency) BSF = (D/(2*d)) * f_s * (1 - (d/D * cos(alpha))^2) = (38.5/15.876) * 24.17 * (1 - 0.2062^2) = 2.425 * 24.17 * 0.9575 = 56.2 Hz

What to look for in the spectrogram

Healthy: Strong tone at 24.17 Hz (shaft), 72.5 Hz (3x), 217.5 Hz (blade-pass 9x) No energy at 86.4 Hz, 131.2 Hz, 56.2 Hz, 9.6 Hz BPFO fault developing: New tone at 86.4 Hz Sidebands at 86.4 +/- 24.17 Hz: 62.2 Hz and 110.6 Hz These sidebands = bearing defect modulating shaft rotation BPFI fault (more serious, inner race rotates): New tone at 131.2 Hz Sidebands at 131.2 +/- 24.17 Hz: 107.0 Hz and 155.4 Hz Cage fault (early warning, very low amplitude): New tone at 9.6 Hz (near DC, hard to detect) Look for amplitude modulation of shaft tone at 9.6 Hz rate
Key point: You must know your bearing model number to calculate these frequencies. Without the bearing geometry, you are doing pattern recognition rather than physics-based diagnosis. Keep a record of every bearing installed in every monitored machine.
G12 — Using Panaural for a real inspection — step-by-step procedure Procedure

Equipment needed

  • Laptop with browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari)
  • USB condenser microphone (built-in laptop mic is usable but less reliable)
  • Firm mounting: tape or magnetic holder to attach mic to pipe near bearing housing
  • Reference tone source: phone app or small speaker playing 440 Hz known signal
  • Hearing protection if operating near loud machinery

Procedure

  1. Mount mic firmly. Mark the position with tape so you can return to exactly the same spot. Position matters for amplitude consistency.
  2. Run reference calibration. Play 440 Hz reference tone near mic for 2 seconds. Record and note the dBFS level at 440 Hz bin. This is your session reference.
  3. Verify self-tests pass. Load the demo. All four self-tests must show PASS before continuing.
  4. Record 3–5 minutes of machine audio. Label the file with date, machine ID, and operating condition (load, speed if variable).
  5. Load WAV into Panaural. Set maxFreq to cover the highest bearing frequency of interest. Adjust dB ceiling until tones are visible (not saturated).
  6. Identify peaks. Compare auto-detected peaks to your kinematic frequency list. Document which peaks match which components.
  7. Compare to baseline. If a baseline recording exists, load it in a second tab. Look for new peaks, shifted frequencies, amplitude changes > 6 dB.
  8. Document findings. Screenshot, note any anomalies, escalate if bearing fault frequencies detected.

Red flags that require immediate escalation

  • New tone at any bearing defect frequency (BPFI, BPFO, BSF, FTF)
  • Any existing tone rising > 6 dB since last measurement
  • Broadband noise floor rising above 1 kHz (possible cavitation or lubrication failure)
  • Shaft frequency shifting (possible speed change or coupling problem)
Escalation rule: Panaural can flag "investigate this." It cannot diagnose "replace bearing 3 now." Any flag must be reviewed by a qualified maintenance engineer before scheduling work. Do not take a machine offline based solely on Panaural output without qualified sign-off.

Self-Test Results

Four synthetic tests run on every page load. No microphone required. Verify the STFT mathematics before trusting Panaural with real data. All four must pass.

Test Result What it verifies Actual output
Synthetic signal methodology — how each test is designed

Test 1 — Pure sine detection

Generate a mathematically perfect 50 Hz sine at 8,000 Hz for 1 second. Run STFT with frameSize=8000 (fftSize=8192). The peak bin must be within ±3 bins of bin 51 (50 × 8192 / 8000 = 51.2). Confirms the FFT algorithm places energy at the correct bin.

Test 2 — Noise robustness (SNR ≈ 10 dB)

Mix the 50 Hz sine with Gaussian noise (Box-Muller, std=0.224, SNR≈10 dB — realistic for a microphone on a pipe with ambient background). The peak must still detect at 50 Hz, confirming the algorithm is robust to real-world recording conditions.

Test 3 — Silence

Feed all-zero samples. No bin may exceed −80 dB. If it does, floating-point accumulation in the FFT introduces numerical noise that would trigger false alarms on quiet signals. The −240 dB observed means the implementation is numerically clean.

Test 4 — Clipping detection

Generate a sine at 2× amplitude, then hard-clip at ±0.9 to simulate ADC saturation. Over 71% of samples will be at the clip boundary. The test checks this is flagged. Clipping introduces harmonic distortion across the entire spectrum — the system must warn rather than silently report a distorted result as real fault peaks.
